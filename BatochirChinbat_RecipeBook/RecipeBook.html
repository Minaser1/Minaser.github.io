Tsuivan

What is "Tsuivan"?

Tsuivan" (often spelled "Цуйван" in Mongolian) is a traditional Mongolian dish that consists of homemade noodles stir-fried with meat (usually mutton or beef) and vegetables. The dish is hearty and filling, making it a staple in Mongolian cuisine, especially in the colder months. "Tsuivan" is a popular dish in Mongolia, often enjoyed as a comforting and satisfying meal. It's relatively simple to make and is a common dish in Mongolian households. The dish reflects the Mongolian way of utilizing available ingredients to create something nourishing and hearty.

Ingredients:

  • 1 cup (240 milliliters) water
  • 2 1/2 cups (320 grams) all-purpose flour plus more for dusting
  • Vegetable oil for rolling and cooking
  • 1 onion peeled and thinly sliced
  • 8 ounces (250 grams) mutton or beef cut into bite-size pieces
  • 2 carrots cut into matchsticks
  • 1 potato peeled and cut into matchsticks
  • 1/4 green cabbage thinly sliced, optional
  • 2 cloves garlic peeled and minced
  • 3/4 cup (177 milliliters) water
  • 2 green onions thinly sliced
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Insructions:

  1. Place the flour in a large bowl and make a well in the center. Add the water and mix to combine and form a dough.
  2. On a lightly floured surface, knead the dough well until smooth and elastic. If too sticky to handle, add a little more flour. If too crumbly, add a little water.
  3. Cover with an inverted bowl or wrap in plastic and allow to rest for 15 minutes.
  4. Divide the rested dough into 4 equal pieces. Cover three of the pieces and place one on a lightly floured surface.
  5. Roll the piece of dough into a thin sheet, making sure the bottom does not stick to the surface.
  6. Grease the top of the sheet with about 1 tablespoon of oil- making sure all parts are covered. Loosely roll the sheet up, long side to long side. Using a sharp knife, quickly cut the roll into strips about 1/4 inch (0.6 cm) wide. Gently shake the strips loose with your fingers to separate and place on a greased baking sheet or other surface. Repeat with remaining dough.
  7. Alternatively, you can cut the oiled sheet of dough in half, stack on top of each other and lightly pan fry on each side until starting to puff, but still pliable. Roll the lightly pan-fried sheets up and cut into strips. This way will make the noodles easier to cut and separate. Repeat with remaining dough and set aside.
  8. Place a large pan over medium heat and drizzle with about a tablespoon of oil.
  9. Once heated, add the sliced onions and cook, stirring often, until softened.
  10. Stir in the meat, season with salt and pepper, and continue to cook, stirring occasionally, until golden on all sides.
  11. Add the carrots, potatoes, cabbage if using, and garlic. Cook until starting to soften.
  12. Pour the water into the pan and bring to a boil. Reduce to a simmer and arrange the sliced noodles around the top, leaving a hole in the center. Drizzle the tops of the noodles evenly with about 1 tablespoon oil. Cover tightly with a lid and steam the noodles until cooked through, about 15 minutes. If the water has evaporated before the noodles are finished cooking, pour in a little more water.
  13. Once cooked, remove the lid and gently fan the the noodles using a paper fan, the pot lid, or even a cutting board to help dry out the top layer.
  14. Use a fork to gently fluff and separate the noodles, then lightly toss all the ingredients together. Season to taste with salt and pepper and serve immediately topped with the green onions.
